# Urgency Inflation: Why Faster AI Can Increase Work

AI tools can make individual tasks faster while *increasing* total workload by shifting norms: what used to be “nice to have” becomes expected, and what used to be “urgent” becomes ambient. This article synthesizes a Spanish-language X thread reflecting on late-night work enabled by AI and connects it to classic efficiency rebound dynamics and modern social-acceleration framing. [1]

## The Claim

The thread’s core argument is not that AI fails to save time per task, but that it changes what people and organizations *treat as urgent*. When “first drafts in 30 seconds” becomes normal, the time savings get reinvested into more work, more variants, and faster response expectations, pushing work later into the day. [1]

The author cites (as reported in the thread) survey-style figures like “only ~21% use saved time for personal life” and “~77% say AI added load,” plus a “2026 NBER study of ~6,000 managers” reporting no clear productivity impact despite high adoption. These should be treated as claims from the thread unless independently verified. [1]

## Mechanisms

### Rebound: From Efficiency to More Output

The thread explicitly uses Jevons-style rebound logic: when something becomes cheaper (time/effort per email, analysis, draft), total usage can rise because new use cases become viable. In knowledge work, “more reports, more analysis, more variants” appear because they newly fit within budget. [1]

### Capacity Becomes Baseline

The thread also updates Parkinson’s Law: instead of work expanding to fill *time available*, it expands to fill *capability available*. If the tool expands capability, expectations expand accordingly—both self-imposed (“I can finish tonight”) and external (“you can turn this around quickly now”). [1]

### Latency Collapse Redefines “Urgent”

A distinct AI-era dynamic is *latency tolerance collapse*: if a system can answer in 30 seconds, waiting 30 minutes starts to feel like neglect. In that environment, “urgent” stops being an exception (something broke) and becomes the default background state (“everything is interruptible”). [1]

## Sociological Lens

The thread frames urgency inflation as the latest accelerator within broader “social acceleration” dynamics (Hartmut Rosa), including “frenetic standstill” (moving faster without progress) and “the shrinking present” (shorter validity horizons for work). It pairs this with Byung-Chul Han’s “achievement society” critique: pressure is internalized as self-exploitation (“I can”), producing a distinct fatigue pattern. It also invokes Jonathan Crary’s “24/7” thesis: if tools never sleep, human unavailability can be treated as arbitrary. [1]

## Practical Countermeasures

The thread proposes three pragmatic moves, reframed here as operational guidelines:

1. **Separate execution speed from decision urgency.** AI collapses execution time, not decision time; preserve deliberate “decision latency.” [1]
2. **Design friction on purpose.** Declare response windows, batch communications, disable notifications, and restore “office hours” for always-available tools. [1]
3. **Measure by outcomes, not throughput.** Prefer fewer high-quality, high-leverage outputs over maximizing “tasks completed.” [1]

## Implications for “AI Productivity” Narratives

This is a micro-level explanation for why “AI productivity paradox” claims can coexist with widespread anecdotes of faster completion: time saved is often converted into expanded scope, expanded communication volume, and higher responsiveness norms rather than fewer working hours. In other words, AI can increase *potential output* faster than organizations can convert it into *measured productivity*—and it can raise perceived urgency faster than it reduces total work. [1]
